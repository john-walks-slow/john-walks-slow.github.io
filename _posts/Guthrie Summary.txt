Moral principles are human constructs and can be evaluated on an empirical basis.

Guthrie assumes that the most desirable code of conduct are those which

- promote human welfare
- contribute to the functioning of social machinery
- allow for the greatest freedom for individual

Including other organisms in our moral system is unsound because

- Animals can't be moral being and amoral being in the same time
- If it is the organism' s pain, in and of itself, that we wish to prevent, we incur unnecessary responsibilities

Applying moral code only to "higher" organism is also unsound.

Discrimination is bad because it is bad taxonomy, not so much because of the moral code.

Our heterotrophic nature precludes any philosophy. There is no basis of equivalency by which we could compare animals' pain with human welfare.

In the process of our self-domestication we have removed ourselves farther and farther from our
contact with other species, and it is because of this lack of firsthand understanding and familiarity that we attribute human feelings and motivations to other organisms. Relating emotionally to other organisms is a natural by-product of the breakdown of our sharp value delineation between humans and nonhumans, resulting from our increasing lack of contact with other organisms.
